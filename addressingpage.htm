In the context of computer networking, a topology refers to the way in which the parts of a network—such as computers, servers, and devices—are arranged and connected to one another. You can think of it as the "map" or "blueprint" of the network. Topologies are generally categorized into two types: Physical Topology: The actual physical layout of the cables, devices, and nodes. Logical Topology: The path that data signals take as they travel through the network, regardless of the physical connections. Here are the most common network topologies:

1. Bus Topology All devices are connected to a single central cable, called the "backbone" or "bus." Data travels along the bus until it reaches the destination node. Pros: Simple to install and inexpensive. Cons: If the main cable fails, the entire network goes down; performance degrades with more devices.

2. Star Topology All nodes are connected individually to a central connection point, typically a switch or hub. This is the most common topology in home and office networks today. Pros: Easy to troubleshoot; if one cable fails, only that device is affected, not the whole network. Cons: If the central switch fails, the entire network fails.
