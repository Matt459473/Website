Back to main page Overview of Transmission Media Transmission media refers to the physical pathways used to carry data signals between devices in a network. They are broadly categorized into Guided (wired) and Unguided (wireless) media.

Fiber OpticGlass or plastic strands that transmit data as pulses of light.Very high bandwidth, immune to EMI, long distance.Backbone networks, transoceanic cables.

2. Unguided Media (Wireless) Unguided media transmits electromagnetic waves through the air or space without a physical conductor. Radio Waves: Omni-directional; used for mobile communications, Wi-Fi (WLAN), and Bluetooth. Microwaves: High-frequency, directional; require line-of-sight. Used for long-distance terrestrial links and satellite communication. Infrared: Short-range, line-of-sight; used in remote controls and short-distance data transfer.

Key Performance Factors When choosing transmission media, consider these critical factors: Bandwidth: The capacity of the medium to transmit data (bits per second). Attenuation: The loss of signal strength over distance. Higher frequencies generally suffer from higher attenuation. Interference (EMI/RFI): Electromagnetic or radio frequency interference that can corrupt signals. Copper is susceptible; fiber optic is immune. Security: How easily can the signal be intercepted? Wired is generally more secure than wireless. Cost: Includes installation, maintenance, and the physical material cost.
